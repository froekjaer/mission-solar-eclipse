**Duration at Palma:** 1 minute 35 seconds. Variation across the island: 80–96 seconds (best at SW coast).

### Critical Observation Geometry

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Sun altitude at totality | **2.1°** |
| Sun azimuth at totality | **310°** (WNW) |
| Constellation | Leo (Regulus ~5° from Sun) |

**⚠️ The single most important operational constraint:** The eclipsed sun is barely above the western horizon. You need a **completely unobstructed view to the WNW**. Any building, tree, mountain, or hill in that direction will block totality. This is not negotiable.

The low altitude creates a **golden hour eclipse** — dramatic orange/amber sky, 360° sunset glow, the eclipsed sun appearing larger due to atmospheric refraction. Visually spectacular, but the narrow viewing angle makes site selection critical.

### Eclipse Path Through Mallorca

The entire island lies within the ~265 km-wide path of totality. The central line passes through southern Mallorca, approximately 39.41°N. The umbral shadow sweeps WNW→ESE at ~3.5 km/s.

### Weather — August 12 Climatology

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Historical clear-sky probability | **75–80%** |
| Average cloud cover (Aug) | 20–30% |
| Completely overcast days (Aug) | 3–5 per month |
| Evening clearing tendency | Strong — sea breeze dies down |

**Weather dynamics:** The Embat (sea breeze) creates cumulus over the Tramuntana mountains during the day but typically dissipates by evening. By 20:30 CEST, convection is collapsing. The south and southwest coasts are in the mountain rain shadow — clearest skies.

**Contingency rule:** On eclipse day, check AEMET/Windy.com forecast at 16:00 CEST. If cloud probability >30% at primary site, drive to the clearest coastal location with WNW sea horizon. Mallorca is small — you can cross the island in <1 hour.

### Visible Phenomena — Priority List

With only 95 seconds of totality, you can't observe everything. Prioritize:

1. **Solar corona** (10 sec) — overall shape, streamers. Solar cycle 25 declining phase means intermediate corona structure with well-developed polar plumes and helmet streamers.
2. **Chromosphere and prominences** (5 sec) — pink ring and flame-like protrusions at the limb. Visible to naked eye.
3. **Venus** (2 sec) — magnitude -3.9, ~20° upper right of the sun. Brilliant.
4. **Regulus** (2 sec) — magnitude +1.4, ~5° from the sun. In the corona field.
5. **360° sunset horizon** (5 sec) — the "sunset in all directions" effect at low altitude.
6. **Diamond ring and Baily's beads** (seconds at C2/C3) — the last/first rays of sunlight.

## 2. Observation Plan

### Site Selection

**Decision framework:** Select observation site on August 12 based on weather forecast and horizon verification. Primary → Fallback → Emergency.

**Site Requirements (all must be met):**

1. Unobstructed WNW horizon (sea preferred, elevated clearing acceptable)
2. Vehicle accessible (rental car on eclipse day)
3. Flat, stable ground for tripod
4. Arrive by 19:00 CEST (T-90 min before C1)
5. Cellular signal for weather updates

### Observation Site Candidates

#### Primary: Port d'Andratx / Sant Elm Area

- **Coordinates:** ~39.55°N, 2.39°E
- **Duration:** ~96 seconds
- **Horizon:** Sea to WNW, unobstructed
- **Access:** 35 min drive from hotel via Ma-1
- **Notes:** Sant Elm beach or elevated viewpoints above the port. Check Google Earth/Street View for exact viewing spot. Arrive early — popular sunset spot.
- **Parking:** Limited in Sant Elm village. Scout parking on reconnaissance.

#### Fallback: Palma — Paseo Marítimo

- **Duration:** ~95 seconds
- **Horizon:** Sea to west-northwest
- **Access:** Walking distance from hotel (AluaSoul Carolina is in Can Pastilla/Playa de Palma area — verify exact location)
- **Notes:** Urban setting, lights may affect adaptation, but sea horizon guaranteed. Easiest logistics.

#### Emergency: South Coast (Colònia de Sant Jordi / Ses Salines)

- **Duration:** ~95 seconds
- **Horizon:** Sea to west/south
- **Access:** ~50 min drive
- **Notes:** If clouds threaten NW/SW, drive south. Flat terrain, multiple pull-off spots along coast road.

### Photography Plan

**Equipment status:** `[TBC]` — Mission Owner to confirm available gear.

For a 1m35s totality with the sun at 2.1°:

**Minimum viable setup:**
- Camera body with manual controls (any DSLR or mirrorless)
- Telephoto lens ≥200mm (300–600mm ideal for corona detail)
- Sturdy tripod
- **Solar filter** — ISO 12312-2 certified Baader AstroSolar ND 5.0 film or equivalent. **This is non-negotiable for eye and sensor safety during partial phases.**
- Remote shutter release or 2-second self-timer

**Recommended shooting sequence (1m35s):**

| Phase | Action | Time |
|---|---|---|
| T-5 min | Final focus check, filter ON, test exposure | 20:27 |
| C1 (19:39) | Partial phase photography (optional) | 19:39–20:31 |
| T-30 sec | Remove filter, start bracketing sequence | 20:31:39 |
| C2 (20:32:09) | **TOTALITY BEGINS** — bracket exposures | — |
| Mid-totality | Corona detail shots | 20:32:57 |
| T-10 sec to C3 | Final wide corona frames | 20:33:35 |
| C3 (20:33:45) | **TOTALITY ENDS** — replace filter | — |
| Post-C3 | Verify filter ON, check images | 20:34 |

**Exposure bracketing for totality:** The corona spans ~14 stops of dynamic range. Shoot a bracket sequence (e.g., 1/2000, 1/500, 1/125, 1/30, 1/8, 1/2, 1s, 2s) at f/8, ISO 400. Adjust for your lens aperture.

**Critical note at 2.1° altitude:** Atmospheric refraction will compress the sun vertically. The corona at such low altitude may show chromatic dispersion (red bottom, blue top). This is a feature, not a bug — it creates unique images. Compose with some sky context to capture the dramatic colors.

### Safety Plan

#### Solar Viewing Safety (Absolute)

1. **ISO 12312-2 certified solar filters mandatory** for all viewing and photography during partial phases (C1→C2 and C3→C4)
2. Filters must be **in front of the lens**, not behind (eyepiece filters are dangerous)
3. Verify filter integrity before use — no scratches, pinholes, or damage
4. Filter OFF only during totality (C2→C3): 1 minute 35 seconds
5. **Filter ON immediately at C3** — practice the timing
6. Never look at the sun through an unfiltered optical viewfinder
7. Use live view / electronic viewfinder with filter for partial phases
8. No sunglasses, smoked glass, or improvised filters — ever

#### General Safety

- **Evening driving:** Eclipse ends near sunset. Drive back in twilight — be alert for pedestrians and wildlife
- **Heat:** August Mallorca averages 31°C. Bring water, hat, sunscreen for the wait
- **Crowds:** Mallorca is a popular eclipse destination. Expect traffic and crowds at known viewing spots. Arrive early
- **Mobile phone:** Save offline maps and eclipse timing app before leaving hotel

```text
Check AEMET/Windy.com forecast
    ├── Primary site (Port d'Andratx) cloud prob <30% → Go primary

    ├── All >50% cloud probability → Consider driving to clearest point
    └── Widespread overcast → Accept; focus on experience over photography
```

### Go/No-Go Decision

This mission has committed resources (flights, hotel). The Go/No-Go decision is about **site selection on eclipse day**, not mission cancellation. The mission proceeds regardless of weather — the observation outcome varies, but the experience and framework learning occur either way.

**No-Go triggers (site-specific, not mission):**
- Safety concern at chosen site (crowds, access blocked, unsafe terrain)
- Equipment failure that prevents safe solar viewing

**Mitigation:** Have at least 3 pre-scouted sites. Mallorca is small — alternatives are within 30-60 minutes.

| ID | Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| R1 | **Cloud cover at observation site** | 20–25% | Critical | Drive to clearest location. Mallorca is small — mobile response. |

Mallorca August is as favorable as European eclipse weather gets. 75-80% clear probability, evening clearing tendency, and the entire island as your observation platform (with a car). The low sun altitude is a challenge for photography but creates a uniquely dramatic visual experience.
